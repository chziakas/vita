Abstract

Vision–Language Models (VLMs) show promise as zero-shot goal-conditioned value functions, but their frozen pre-trained representations limit generalization and temporal reasoning. We introduce VITA, a zero-shot value function learning method that enhances both capabilities via test-time adaptation. At inference, a lightweight adaptation module is updated via a gradient step on a meta-learned self-supervised loss, such that each test-time update improves value estimation. By updating sequentially over a trajectory, VITA encodes history into its parameters, addressing the temporal reasoning limitations. To mitigate shortcut learning, we propose a dissimilarity-based sampling strategy that selects semantically diverse segments of the trajectory during training.

Method

Dissimilarity-Based Sampling

Expert visual trajectories contain consecutive frames that are highly redundant, causing the model to overfit to frequently occurring late-stage visual patterns. To address this, we propose a dissimilarity-based sampling strategy that increases intra-batch variance and acts as a form of importance sampling, emphasizing underrepresented but semantically meaningful segments of the trajectory.

Training framework. Dissimilarity-based sampling selects semantically diverse segments to prevent overfitting to redundant consecutive frames.
